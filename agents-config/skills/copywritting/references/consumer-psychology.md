# Consumer Psychology

The deep desire engine behind any high-converting copy or email: which biological want it taps, where the reader is in their journey, and which psychological lever to pull. Use this to make an email feel personal, urgent, and impossible not to act on.

## The Life-Force 8 (LF8)

Humans are born wanting these eight things. They are the strongest, least-resistible drivers in persuasion. Tie the core promise to at least one.

1. Survival, enjoyment of life, life extension.
2. Enjoyment of food and beverages.
3. Freedom from fear, pain, and danger.
4. Sexual companionship.
5. Comfortable living conditions.
6. To be superior, winning, keeping up with the Joneses.
7. Care and protection of loved ones.
8. Social approval.

For a developer/creator/founder audience, the live levers are usually: superiority and status (6), social approval (8), comfortable living / financial security (5), and freedom from fear (3 - falling behind, being replaced, wasting years on the wrong path).

## The 9 Learned (Secondary) Wants

Acquired, not biological, so weaker. Reinforce with them, never lead with them.

To be informed, curiosity, cleanliness, efficiency, convenience, dependability/quality, beauty/style, economy/profit, bargains.

Rule: lead with an LF8 desire, support with learned wants (efficiency, profit, quality), justify with logic.

## The Desire Mechanism

Persuasion always runs: `Tension (a triggered LF8 lever) -> desire -> action to satisfy it`. The first use of any product happens inside the reader's mind. Make them feel the satisfaction before they buy: have them mentally use the product, feel the relief, then convince them it is *your* product that delivers it (proof + guarantee + credibility).

## The 5 Buying Stages

Write to where the reader actually is. Cold copy that assumes readiness fails.

1. Pre-contemplation: does not know you exist or that they have the problem.
2. Contemplation: aware of the product, thinks it might help one day.
3. Preparation: considering buying, needs more info on benefits/advantages.
4. Action: becomes a customer.
5. Maintenance: keep the product woven into their life so the problem always recalls you.

A single email can touch all five; a sequence can walk the reader from stage 1 to 5 over time.

## The 17 Persuasion Principles

Diagnostic levers, not decorations. Pick the few that fit the offer and reader.

1. **Fear / sell the scare.** Fear drives action, but only when the product is a real remedy. Works only if all four hold: it scares them, offers a specific recommendation, the action is seen as effective, and they believe they can do it. Deadlines and scarcity are the everyday form.
2. **Ego morphing.** Give the product an identity the reader wants to claim; appeal to vanity around status, intelligence, success, attractiveness.
3. **Transfer (borrowed credibility).** Associate with symbols, people, or institutions the reader already respects; their trust transfers to you, and they question your claims less.
4. **Bandwagon / belonging.** Show the group they join. Three group types: aspirational (want to be like), associative (share values), dissociative (want to avoid). Make aspirational figures easy to identify with.
5. **Means-end chain.** Sell tomorrow's end result, not today's features. People buy the bottom-line outcome, so lead the copy and imagery with the positive end state.
6. **Step-by-step (awareness stages).** Match the message to the buying stage above; inform fully, or sequence ads/emails from stage 1 to 5.
7. **Inoculation (lifetime preference).** Warn of an incoming attack, make a weak version of it, then arm a strong defense. Teach them to filter competitors through your lens; invite comparison to signal confidence.
8. **Belief re-ranking.** Don't try to replace a belief wholesale; reinforce or gently undermine an existing one with emotion (right brain) and facts (left brain). Easier and more durable.
9. **Central vs peripheral route.** Deep thinkers persuade themselves through logic and defend the decision as their own; light thinkers respond to pleasant cues and images. Serve both.
10. **The 6 weapons of influence:** reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof, liking, authority, scarcity. (See below.)
11. **Message organization / simplicity.** Write so anyone can grasp it instantly; clarity beats cleverness.
12. **Examples beat statistics.** Concrete examples relate to personal experience and take less effort to process than raw stats. Use a vivid example, then back it with a number.
13. **Message sidedness.** Compliment what's good about alternatives, then show why you're better. Two-sided honesty reads as fair and raises trust.
14. **Repetition and redundancy.** It takes many touches to land (think ~7). Repetition = same message again; redundancy = varied versions of the same message. Use redundancy.
15. **Rhetorical questions.** A statement disguised as a question. It grabs attention and makes the reader engage with the claim. "Wouldn't it be easier if...?"
16. **Evidence.** Facts, objects, and third-party opinions convince even superficial readers. Present evidence clearly and simply.
17. **Heuristics (lazy brains).** People shortcut decisions. Length-implies-strength: longer copy and many testimonials signal "there must be something to it." Quantity of proof creates certainty.

## The 6 Weapons of Influence (detail)

- **Reciprocity:** give real value first; people feel pulled to give back.
- **Commitment / consistency:** elicit a chain of small "yes" responses; momentum snowballs toward the buy.
- **Social proof:** people copy people like them; show many users, not one.
- **Liking:** readers act for people they feel connected to; be a real human, show personality.
- **Authority:** credentials, expertise, recognizable endorsements.
- **Scarcity:** people want most what they might lose; fear of loss beats hope of gain, but the reason for scarcity must be real.

## Email-Specific Cues

- The **P.S. is read first** by many; restate the offer, the deadline, and the action there.
- Open emotional (LF8 lever), close logical (proof to justify).
- Make the reader feel the result before the ask.
- Use rhetorical questions and the mirror effect to make the reader feel the email is about them.
- One email, one core idea, one action.

## How To Apply

- Before writing, name the single LF8 lever the promise pulls. If you can only name a learned want, the promise is too weak.
- Diagnose the buying stage and match the job (educate vs convert).
- Keep only the two strongest arguments; weak arguments drag down strong ones.
- Lead emotional, justify logical, prove with examples then numbers.
