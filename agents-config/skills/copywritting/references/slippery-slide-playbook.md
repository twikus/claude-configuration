# Slippery-Slide Copy Playbook

Long-form persuasion: make every element pull the reader into the next, sell the concept before the product, and close objections before the CTA.

## Master Principle

The copy is a slippery slide: every visual, headline, sentence, paragraph, proof point, and CTA exists to make the next element feel natural to consume. If a line does not create curiosity, clarity, desire, proof, or action, cut it.

Use this question while editing: `Does this sentence make the next sentence easier to read?`

## Product And Prospect Nature

Before writing, understand two natures:
- Product nature: why this product exists, why now, what is naturally desirable about it, what category expectations it must harmonize with, and what makes it different.
- Prospect nature: their current awareness, emotional state, vocabulary, objections, prior purchases, desired identity, and the real job they are trying to get done.

Strong copy starts from the market's existing desire. Do not invent a desire from scratch; connect the product to something the prospect already wants.

## Sell The Concept First

Do not start by selling the object. Sell the concept, the shift, the new way to see the problem.

Useful patterns:
- `Old belief -> hidden cost -> new belief -> product as bridge`
- `Familiar concept -> surprising connection -> product becomes obvious`
- `Problem they tolerate -> consequence they do not want -> simple mechanism`

Example move: instead of selling a course, sell the idea that a developer can stop collecting random tutorials and build one real project with a structured path.

## The 31 Psychological Buying Points

Use these as diagnostic lenses, not as manipulative decorations.

1. Honesty: admit limits, tradeoffs, and who the product is not for.
2. Integrity: keep tone aligned with reality; do not overpromise.
3. Credibility: use proof, mechanism, details, and clear reasoning.
4. Value proof: show why the outcome is worth more than the price.
5. Price justification: explain price when the reader starts wondering about it.
6. Greed / good deal: show economic upside or avoided waste, without cheapening the brand.
7. Authority: credentials, experience, teaching, clients, evidence, or technical command.
8. Risk reversal: guarantee, trial, refund, demo, proof, or transparent constraints.
9. Product nature: write in harmony with the category and expectation.
10. Prospect nature: speak to the exact person, not a broad demographic.
11. Trend / mode: surf current attention when relevant; avoid stale positioning.
12. Timing: use market timing, current pain, seasonal urgency, or product maturity.
13. Linking: connect the product to known ideas, tools, events, or identities.
14. Consistency: make the first commitment easy; later actions become easier.
15. Harmonizing: make the offer feel natural for this buyer, this moment, this market.
16. Belonging: show the group the buyer joins.
17. Collecting: useful for product suites, cohorts, badges, templates, libraries.
18. Curiosity: create unanswered but relevant questions.
19. Urgency: give a real reason to act now.
20. Fear: use carefully; sell a remedy for an active pain, not vague prevention.
21. Gratification: make the first benefit feel immediate.
22. Exclusivity / rarity / uniqueness: explain who gets access and why it is limited.
23. Simplicity: one promise, one product, one CTA where possible.
24. Human relationship: make the reader feel there is a real person behind the offer.
25. Storytelling: use a concrete journey, discovery, failure, or customer story.
26. Mental engagement: let readers reach some conclusions themselves.
27. Guilt / reciprocity: give enough value that action feels fair, never coercive.
28. Specificity: numbers, concrete details, exact situations, named mechanisms.
29. Familiarity: use known language, analogies, and recognizable patterns.
30. Hope: show a plausible better future without promising impossible outcomes.
31. Satisfaction conviction: make the reader feel they will be happy after acting.

## Format Rules

Use form as part of persuasion:
- Images and first visual impressions act like headlines. They must create curiosity or instant comprehension.
- Paragraph headings can exist mostly to break text and restart attention.
- Typography must be readable and match the brand feeling.
- Long copy is acceptable when the product is expensive, complex, risky, or belief-changing.
- Simple products often need richer emotional explanation; complex products often need simpler explanation.
- Add CTA when the reader has enough arguments to act; do not hide the action.

## Objections

When an objection appears in the reader's mind and the copy ignores it, trust drops.

Common objections:
- Will this work for someone like me?
- Is this worth the price?
- Why should I trust you?
- Why now?
- What happens if I fail?
- Is there a cheaper/free alternative?
- Is this too complex, too long, or too risky?
- Have people like me succeeded with this?

Answer with:
- specificity,
- mechanism,
- proof,
- comparison,
- honest limitation,
- guarantee,
- or a concrete next step.

## Price

Mention price when the reader naturally asks about it. If price is high, justify through value, mechanism, service, guarantee, support, speed, or avoided cost. If price is low, justify why quality is still credible.

Good price framing:
- compare against a familiar alternative,
- quantify time saved or mistake avoided,
- show the cost of doing nothing,
- recap what is included,
- make the next step feel low risk.

## Editing Pass

Run this pass before final delivery:
- Remove throat-clearing.
- Replace abstract claims with concrete images.
- Replace `we/I` with `you` when the reader benefit is missing.
- Cut weak proof and weak arguments.
- Make the first line sharper.
- Make every CTA direct.
- Keep the same voice unless asked to change it.
